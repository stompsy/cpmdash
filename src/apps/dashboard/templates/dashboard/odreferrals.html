The Revolving Door

Every repeat overdose represents a missed opportunity. When patients cycle back through the system, it's rarely random — it's a signal. Treatment fell through. Incarceration interrupted continuity. Housing collapsed. A partner relapsed. Maybe we never connected with them in the first place.

This timeline reveals failure patterns. Clusters show when the system breaks down. Rapid recurrence (days or weeks) suggests immediate post-reversal gaps — no warm handoff, no MAT access, no stable housing. Longer intervals might indicate treatment dropout or life disruption events. Each vertical line is a person we need to reach differently.

What to Do About It

Identify high-frequency patients — those with 3+ overdoses warrant intensive case management, not just episodic response.

Map time intervals — rapid recurrence suggests acute system gaps. Target immediate post-reversal follow-up within 24-48 hours.

Coordinate upstream — connect with jails, detox facilities, and SUD providers to maintain continuity when patients transition.

Build persistent relationships — trust accumulates across encounters. The fifth contact might be the one that sticks.

How Narcan Changed Everything

BEFORE HD NARCAN:

EMS arrives first → ventilate → establish IV → carefully titrate 0.4mg doses → gradual awakening → transport → treatment advocacy. Engagement window: wide open.

HD NARCAN:

Bystander gives 4mg spray → patient wakes in precipitated withdrawal → vomiting, agitated, combative → EMS arrives to refusal → no transport. Engagement window: slammed shut.

NOW:

{# Footer Insights #}

Adapting Your Response Strategy

Accept the new reality: High-dose bystander Narcan isn't going away. Patients will be in withdrawal when you arrive. Plan for it.

Shift to post-reversal follow-up: If you can't engage at the scene, build a 24-48 hour follow-up protocol. Catch them when withdrawal subsides.

Partner with distributors: Work with needle exchanges and harm reduction orgs to teach optimal dosing (start low, go slow when possible).

Track refusal patterns: Correlate bystander-first cases with transport refusals to quantify the engagement gap and justify CPM follow-up resources.

Build trust over time: Accept that first contact post-withdrawal will be rocky. The relationship accumulates across encounters.
