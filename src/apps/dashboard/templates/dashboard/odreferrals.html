{# PORT Introduction Section #}

Every overdose referral is a moment where the system either steps in or steps back. This page pulls together what the Post Overdose Response Team (PORT) sees in the field: when overdoses happen, where they cluster, who reverses them, and whether the same person comes back. The goal isn't a tidy story — it's an honest one that helps community paramedics, partners, and the public understand what is working, what isn't, and where the next opportunity to save a life probably lives.

{# Key benefits list #}
{# Rapid Response Impact #}
Rapid Response Impact.
In the 12 months following PORT's launch, fatal overdose rates in Clallam County dropped from 73.3 to 34.8 per 100,000 — roughly a 50% reduction. We can't claim PORT alone caused that drop — the national age-adjusted overdose death rate also fell 26.2% in the same 2023–2024 window (CDC NCHS Data Brief 549), driven largely by a 35.6% drop in fentanyl-involved deaths and by community naloxone saturation. But the Clallam decline is roughly double the national one, and consistent post-overdose follow-up is one of the few local interventions that plausibly explains that gap.
{# Partnership Networks #}
Partnership Networks.
Knowing which agencies and bystanders are flagging overdoses tells us where the safety net is holding and where it has holes. The same charts that look like "operational metrics" to staff are, for partners, a map of where outreach, MOUD access, housing, and warm handoffs need to be tightened next.
{# State & National Alignment #}
State & National Alignment.
Several patterns you'll see below — declining fatal-overdose rates, repeat overdoses concentrating in the days after a first event, and the shift from EMS-administered to bystander-administered naloxone — match what CDC, NIDA, and peer-reviewed research are documenting nationwide and what the Washington State Department of Health is reporting at the state level. Where we can, we link the supporting sources at the bottom of this page.
{# Honest Limits #}
Honest Limits.
Everything here only counts overdoses PAFD knows about. Unwitnessed events, private reversals, and people who refuse contact don't appear. We point this out so the charts inform decisions without being mistaken for the whole picture.

Overdose Density by Day & Hour

When overdoses actually happen vs. when CPMs are typically on the clock

{{ fig_density_map|safe }}

Reading the Heatmap

Each cell counts how many overdoses occurred during a specific weekday-and-hour combination across the entire dataset. Darker cells are louder hours; lighter cells are quieter ones. The yellow rectangle outlines a standard CPM daytime shift block (Mon–Fri, 08:00–18:00). Anything outside that box is happening on evenings, nights, or weekends — when fewer staff are typically on scene.

The factual takeaway is straightforward: overdose risk does not follow a 9-to-5 schedule. Weekday afternoons and early evenings tend to light up, but so do late nights and weekends, when most clinics and outreach offices are closed. The rectangle is a visual reminder that even an "extended" daytime shift leaves real gaps in coverage.

What it might be telling us: hot weekday afternoon hours could reflect the moment people leave work, schools, or court dates and reconnect with using partners — a window where a check-in call, a peer drop-by, or a stocked naloxone supply could prevent an event. Weekend and overnight clusters likely lean on bystander reversals; that is where strengthening bystander naloxone access, hotel and shelter partnerships, and on-call CPM follow-up the next morning probably matters most. Quiet cells aren't necessarily safe — they may simply reflect when overdoses are less likely to be witnessed or reported.

The Revolving Door

A repeat overdose is rarely random. The headline numbers above tell you how many patients we have responded to more than once, how soon those second events tend to occur, and what share of all overdoses come from people we have already met. The lines below show the same data person-by-person so the pattern becomes visible.

What we can say from the data: repeats cluster more in the first weeks after an initial event than at any other interval, the people with three or more events make up a small slice of patients but a disproportionate share of total overdoses, and the gap between someone's first and second event has not stayed constant year over year.

What it likely points to: tight clusters early on are most consistent with broken handoffs — no follow-up appointment that stuck, no MOUD induction, a return to the same housing situation or using partner. Longer gaps followed by a new event are more consistent with treatment dropout, a life shock (lost housing, lost job, lost relationship), or a change in the local drug supply. We can't prove cause from these views, but they are good places to look first. Nationally, the elevated-risk window immediately after a non-fatal overdose is well documented — Larochelle et al. (Annals of Internal Medicine, 2018) found that patients started on buprenorphine or methadone after a non-fatal overdose had roughly half the all-cause mortality of those who received neither medication.

Reading the trend

Each point is a quarter. The colored lines track how often a patient seen in that quarter had another overdose within 30, 90, 180, or 365 days. The light neutral line is a 3-quarter moving average, so it smooths out single-quarter spikes and shows the underlying direction. A line breaks on the right where the window hasn't fully elapsed yet — for example, the 365-day series simply can't be calculated for last quarter. That's the calendar, not a data loss.

Plausibly: the 2024 ramp-up is mostly an artifact of better referral capture as PORT matured rather than people actually overdosing more. The flatter or lower levels after late 2024 are more likely to reflect real change — improved follow-up, better naloxone saturation, or a shift in supply — but quarter-to-quarter noise is real, and a single quarter shouldn't be read as a turning point on its own.

Repeat Rates Since 2024 Q4

Focused view of the post-ramp-up period with a dedicated trend line.

{{ fig_repeat_overdose_quarterly_post_ramp_trend|safe }}

Reading this view

Same data as above, but the early collection ramp-up is removed and the series starts at 2024 Q4 — the point where referral capture had clearly stabilized. The solid light line is a short moving average; the dashed line is a simple linear trend fit only to this post-ramp window so you can see whether the underlying repeat rate is drifting up, down, or sideways.

Worth thinking about: a flat or downward slope here, against stable case volume, is the cleanest signal we currently have that follow-up and warm handoffs are bending the curve. It would be premature to call it victory — a few quarters is still a small sample and the local drug supply can change quickly — but if the slope reverses sharply, that is a real prompt to revisit outreach intensity.

Cumulative Repeat-Overdose Rate (Kaplan-Meier)

By day t after a patient's first observed overdose, what fraction have had a second one - adjusted for how long we've actually been able to watch each patient.

{{ fig_repeat_overdose_km|safe }}

How to read this

A Kaplan-Meier curve answers a fair question: by X days after someone's first observed overdose, what share of patients have had a second one? It accounts for the fact that we have not been watching everyone for the same amount of time — recent patients haven't had a chance to relapse yet, and the math handles that honestly. Each upward step is one real patient. The shaded band is a 95% confidence interval; it shrinks as more patients have been observed for longer.

What it suggests: the curve rising fastest in the first weeks is the clearest argument for front-loading effort — the period right after a reversal is when the next event is most likely. This pattern matches national survival analyses of post-overdose cohorts (see Larochelle 2018). A slope that flattens out earlier each cohort year (rather than the cumulative curve ever going down) is what "intervention working" actually looks like in survival data, even if it doesn't feel as dramatic as a falling line.

{# Footer Insights - colored bar, no vertical line or icon #}

What This Timeline Is Really Saying

Each horizontal line on the main chart is one patient, and each dot on that line is one overdose. A line with a single dot is a one-time crisis we responded to. A line with several dots is a person whose overdoses have become a pattern. The aligned, seasonal, histogram, and Kaplan-Meier views beneath it are just different ways of asking the same question: how often does the second event come, and how soon?

Across the views, two facts hold up consistently: a relatively small group of patients accounts for a large share of total overdoses, and second events are most likely in the days and weeks immediately after the first. That is not unique to Clallam County — it shows up in published opioid-use-disorder research nationwide — but seeing it in local data sharpens the case for fast, persistent follow-up rather than a single post-event visit.

A reasonable hypothesis, supported by what CPMs see on the ground: tightly-clustered repeats usually trace back to gaps in continuity — no MOUD induction within days of the reversal, no follow-up that the patient could realistically keep, or a discharge back into the same physical environment that produced the first event. Longer-gap repeats are more often tied to life shocks (lost housing, lost relationship, lost job) or a change in what is locally available on the street. Both patterns point in the same direction for the public and for staff: the highest-leverage move is staying in relationship across time, not just being present at the worst moment.

How to Read This Hotspot Map

Each marker is a real overdose that PORT was dispatched to at a specific address — an apartment complex, a motel, a shelter, a park, a parking lot. When markers stack at the same place, that's a hotspot: a location the data says is producing more events than the rest of the map.

The numbers up top come straight from this dataset: {{ hotspot_stats.unique_locations }} unique geocoded locations, with the single highest-volume address accounting for {{ hotspot_stats.top_location_share }}% of mapped responses. Some clusters are unsurprising — known encampments, high-turnover motels, busy intersections. Others are more telling: a single residential address that appears again and again, a quiet block, or a small business that has quietly become a gathering point.

What it likely means in practice: a recurring address rarely reflects "a bad building" so much as a stable mix of people, supply, and circumstances that keeps producing risk in the same place. That makes hotspots high-leverage targets for very specific moves — a conversation with a property manager, a stocked naloxone box at the front desk, a recurring CPM walk-by, or a coordinated outreach time with a partner agency. The map's job is to point that work somewhere concrete instead of "everywhere."

A note on privacy: markers are positioned at the address level. We deliberately avoid identifying individual people, and individual incidents should not be inferred from a single dot.

Garnett MF, Miño AM. Drug Overdose Deaths in the United States, 2023–2024. The local Clallam decline aligns with, and outpaces, the national trajectory.
Washington State overdose data

Post-overdose mortality risk & MOUD
Larochelle MR, Bernson D, Land T, et al. Medication for Opioid Use Disorder After Nonfatal Opioid Overdose and Association With Mortality. Ann Intern Med. 2018;169(3):137–145. acpjournals.org/doi/10.7326/M17-3107 Patients started on buprenorphine or methadone after a non-fatal overdose had roughly half the all-cause mortality of those who received neither – the strongest published evidence for fast MOUD linkage in the post-overdose window the timeline charts visualize.
OTC naloxone & bystander reversal
U.S. Food & Drug Administration. FDA Approves First Over-the-Counter Naloxone Nasal Spray. March 29, 2023. fda.gov – OTC Narcan approval Regulatory inflection point that maps to the shift from EMS-administered to bystander-administered naloxone visible in the Narcan section above.
Community-level overdose response
HEALing Communities Study Consortium. Community-Based Cluster-Randomized Trial to Reduce Opioid Overdose Deaths. N Engl J Med. 2024;391(11):989–1001. pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38884347 Multi-site randomized trial of community-based overdose interventions; the model and the menu of evidence-based practices it tested overlap substantially with what PORT does on a smaller scale.
NIDA overdose trend hub
National Institute on Drug Abuse. Drug Overdose Deaths: Facts and Figures. nida.nih.gov – Overdose Death Rates Visual time series of US overdose deaths by drug class, sex, and year – useful for sanity-checking local trends against the national curve.
