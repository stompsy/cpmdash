The Story the Numbers Tell

Opioid overdose is rarely an isolated event. Across our 130-patient opioid OD cohort and 36-patient general SUD cohort, the data paints a picture of layered, interacting conditions that defy single-issue treatment models. Nearly two-thirds of all patients have a diagnosed behavioral health disorder. More than half of the OD cohort carries chronic physical illness. And roughly one in four opioid overdose patients also has alcohol use disorder — a combination that doesn't just double the risk, it multiplies it.

This page is a systematic walk through every meaningful correlation in the dataset. It's organized into four sections: behavioral health prevalence, chronic illness burden, overdose risk factors, and substance co-occurrence. Each section includes the charts, the numbers, and — more importantly — what they actually mean for clinical practice and program design.

Only 23.8% of opioid overdose patients have zero comorbidities. Three out of four are managing multiple interacting conditions. The question isn't whether integrated care is needed — it's how quickly programs can pivot to deliver it.

The Hidden Majority

Nearly 7 in 10 opioid overdose patients have a diagnosed behavioral health disorder. That's not a coincidence — it's a structural reality. Depression, anxiety, PTSD, and schizophrenia aren't just comorbidities; for many of these patients, they're the root cause that led to self-medication with opioids in the first place.

70% of BH-positive patients have MULTIPLE conditions (mean 2.14 per patient, maximum 5). This isn't a population dealing with single diagnoses — it's a population carrying stacked, interacting mental health burdens where treating one condition without addressing the others is like bailing water from one end of a sinking boat.

Why This Matters for Patient Care

When a community paramedic shows up at a patient's door, they are almost never dealing with "just" an opioid problem. The depression that fuels isolation. The anxiety that prevents showing up to appointments. The schizophrenia that makes medication adherence almost impossible without structured support. These aren't separate conditions — they're one interlocking system, and treating any single piece in isolation is setting patients up to cycle back through the emergency department.

The age-bracket data reveals something the overall averages hide: BH prevalence peaks sharply at 35-44 before dipping at 45-54. That mid-life dip could reflect survivor bias — the most severely affected patients dying or becoming institutionalized before they reach their 50s — or a generational gap in diagnosis rates. Either way, it means the 25-44 age range is where targeted BH intervention would yield the highest return.

The Burden Stacking Problem

More than half of opioid overdose patients carry chronic physical illness alongside their substance use disorder. Hepatitis C leads (a direct consequence of IV drug use), followed by a cluster of cardiovascular conditions — heart failure, hypertension, coronary artery disease — that rarely get addressed in SUD treatment settings. These patients aren't dealing with one problem. They're managing three or four simultaneously, often with fractured care from providers who only see one piece of the puzzle.

10.8% of patients carry the "triple burden" — behavioral health disorder, chronic physical illness, AND alcohol use disorder. These 14 patients represent the most complex cases in the system, and they're almost certainly the highest utilizers of emergency services. The complexity funnel below shows that only about 1 in 4 OD patients has zero comorbidities at all.

Implications for Care Coordination

The chronic illness data tells the same story as the behavioral health data from a different angle. Hepatitis C, heart failure, COPD — these are conditions that require consistent primary care. But patients juggling untreated mental illness and active substance use disorder rarely have the bandwidth for routine lab work and cardiology follow-ups. Community paramedicine sits in the gap: connecting the behavioral health referral, confirming the patient took their metformin, and making sure the Hep C treatment didn't fall through the cracks.

The complexity funnel is the chart you show a funder. It proves that "opioid overdose response" is a misnomer — what community paramedicine actually does is manage multi-system patients who fall through the cracks of specialty care. Only a quarter of these patients have a single problem. Everyone else needs someone connecting the dots.

The 19-Point Gap

79% of repeat overdose patients have a diagnosed behavioral health disorder, compared to 60% of those with a single overdose. That 19-percentage-point gap isn't subtle — it's a screaming signal that untreated or under-treated mental health conditions are a primary driver of recurrent overdose. Depression makes recovery feel pointless. Anxiety prevents showing up to treatment. Schizophrenia fractures the cognitive stability needed for medication adherence.

100% of intentional overdose patients have behavioral health disorders. All 6 patients with documented intentional OD carry BH diagnoses, and 4 of the 6 have suicidal ideation. This isn't recreational overdose gone wrong — it's self-harm via a pharmacological vector, and it demands suicide-specific intervention protocols, not just standard OD reversal.

What This Means for Overdose Response

The standard post-reversal protocol — administer Narcan, transport to ED, discharge with a pamphlet — treats overdose as an acute event. But for the 79% of repeat-overdose patients who have behavioral health disorders, the overdose isn't the disease. It's a symptom of a system that hasn't addressed the depression driving the self-medication, the PTSD making sobriety feel unbearable, or the schizophrenia preventing engagement with treatment programs.

The intentional OD profile is the chart that changes the conversation. When you show a room full of first responders that every single intentional overdose patient has a behavioral health disorder, and two-thirds have suicidal ideation, you're not talking about drug overdose anymore. You're talking about suicide prevention. That requires different training, different protocols, and different follow-up timelines.

The Polysubstance Reality

Nearly 1 in 4 opioid overdose patients also has diagnosed alcohol use disorder. This isn't a footnote — it's a clinical emergency multiplier. Alcohol potentiates opioid-induced respiratory depression, meaning these patients aren't just at higher risk of overdose; they're at higher risk of fatal overdose. Referral pathways that treat OUD and AUD as separate problems are splitting an indivisible risk.

Males have 2x the AUD rate (24.7% vs 13.5%). And AUD prevalence climbs relentlessly with age — from 7.7% in the 18-24 bracket to 38.5% in 55-64 year-olds. This isn't young people partying; it's decades of escalating alcohol dependency compounding the opioid risk. The age trajectory has direct implications for which patients need dual-disorder screening at intake.

Referral Pathway Implications

Referral agencies need to know that the patient they're receiving is unlikely to have "just" an opioid problem. When a quarter of OUD patients also have AUD, and more than half have chronic physical illness on top of behavioral health disorders, the referral isn't just a warm handoff — it's a transfer of a complex, multi-system patient who needs integrated services. Single-issue treatment programs will see these patients bounce back.

The substance-specific BH data is a wake-up call for non-opioid programs too. Methamphetamine patients have 67% BH prevalence — and meth combined with cannabis hits 100%. The methamphetamine epidemic doesn't have a reversal agent like Narcan. These patients present with agitation, psychosis, and cardiovascular crisis, requiring different agency partnerships and intervention strategies than the opioid-focused treatment ecosystem.

What This Means

The data makes a clear case for integrated, multi-system care. Community paramedicine isn't treating overdoses — it's managing a population where substance use disorder, behavioral health conditions, chronic physical illness, and alcohol use disorder interlock into a self-reinforcing cycle that no single-intervention program can break.

The practical implications are specific. Post-reversal protocols should include mandatory BH screening (79% of repeat OD patients need it). Referral pathways should assume polysubstance complexity by default. AUD screening should be standard for all OUD patients over 35. Intentional OD patients need suicide-specific follow-up, not standard OD protocols. And the 14 patients carrying the triple burden of BH + chronic illness + AUD should probably have dedicated case management — they're almost certainly driving a disproportionate share of emergency utilization.

The question has never been whether these patients are complex. The question is whether the system is designed to match that complexity — or whether it's still treating each condition in its own silo, watching patients cycle through ERs and jails because nobody connected the dots.
