{# Era 1: Traditional EMS #}

Pre-Narcan Saturation

1. EMS arrives first on scene

2. Ventilate & establish IV access

3. Carefully titrate low-dose Narcan

4. Gradual awakening

Outcome

High transport rate. Treatment advocacy possible.

{# Era 2: High Dose Era #}

High-Dose Era

1. Bystanders give 4mg+ spray

2. Wakes in precipitated withdrawal

3. Vomiting, agitated, combative

4. EMS arrives to refusal (AMA)

Outcome

No transport. Engagement window closed.

CPM Augmentation

1. CPMs arrive to advocate

2. Help manage withdrawal symptoms

3. Call SUD pros for warm handoff

4. Evaluate treatment options

Outcome

Continuity restored. 70%+ linkage to care.

Reading the Narcan Story in the Data

The chart tracks who administered Narcan at each incident — EMS, law enforcement, a friend, a family member, a coworker, or the patient themselves.

Before Narcan was widely available in the community, overdose response was essentially a one-actor event. EMS arrived, controlled the reversal, and had a real shot at advocating for transport. The protocol worked not because EMS is magic, but because they got to set the pace — careful dose titration, gradual awakening, a conversation that could happen before anyone was deep in withdrawal.

That changed once 4 mg intranasal Narcan went mainstream — a shift the FDA's March 2023 OTC approval of Narcan turned into a flood. Reversal now often happens before the ambulance arrives, which is genuinely good — faster reversal keeps people alive. But a high single dose can throw someone into precipitated withdrawal: vomiting, agitation, and a very strong urge to get away from everyone on scene. EMS arrives to find a patient who is awake, hostile, and refusing transport. On these charts that shows up as bystander administrations climbing and a widening gap between “reversed“ and “transported,“ with more AMA refusals along the way. It isn't a failure of compassion — it's the medication doing exactly what it was designed to do, just in someone's living room or on the street, instead of the back of a rig.

The CPM program is a solution for that gap. When someone refuses transport after a bystander reversal, the connection window doesn't close — it moves. A CPM responds to the scene or follows up as soon as possible after the incident, when the physical misery has settled and the person is more reachable: a warm handoff to a SUD counselor, a prescriber willing to start Suboxone, a follow-up call the next morning. Sustaining the drop in fatal overdoses probably depends less on faster scene response and more on what happens in those hours after the reversal — which is exactly where CPM augmentation is built to plug in.
