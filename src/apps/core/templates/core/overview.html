Meeting residents where they are

Community paramedicine isn't about a single intervention or a checklist. It's about showing up—sometimes in the middle of a crisis, sometimes in the quiet moments of chronic illness—and staying present until we've connected someone to the care they actually need. Port Angeles residents face complex barriers: lack of insurance, transportation challenges, past trauma with the healthcare system, and the isolation that comes from living paycheck to paycheck in a small coastal town. Our job is to bridge those gaps.

Operation Shielding Hope: Post-Overdose Response

When someone overdoses in Port Angeles, the clock starts ticking. Not just for their immediate survival—though that's critical—but for the window of receptivity that opens in the aftermath of a near-death experience. That's when our PORT (Post-Overdose Response Team) deploys alongside EMS. We arrive with medication-assisted treatment, harm reduction supplies, and most importantly, time. We don't rush. We sit with people in their worst moments, help manage withdrawal symptoms right there on scene, and start building trust before the sirens stop echoing.

The results speak to the power of that human connection: among patients who engage with our PORT team, repeat overdoses in the following weeks drop dramatically. It's not magic—it's consistency, empathy, and refusing to let anyone fall through the cracks between emergency response and long-term recovery. Every person we reach is someone's parent, child, or friend. We treat them that way.

Clinical Care & Navigation: Meeting Chronic Needs

Not every crisis arrives with sirens. Many of the residents we serve are managing diabetes, heart disease, or COPD in homes without reliable transportation or the ability to take time off work for medical appointments. They're dealing with medication lists they don't understand, wounds that won't heal, and the grinding stress of trying to stay healthy while barely staying housed. That's where our home visit program creates space for healing.

We show up at their door with a blood pressure cuff, wound care supplies, and the expertise to catch problems before they become emergency room visits. We reconcile medications, teach diabetes management, coordinate telehealth appointments with specialists, and ensure that when someone does need to see a doctor, they arrive with context—not just a symptom list scrawled on the back of an envelope. Most importantly, we make warm handoffs. We don't just refer people to services; we walk them through the door, introduce them to the clinician, and follow up to make sure the connection holds.

This work is about dignity. About treating a 68-year-old veteran with congestive heart failure like a whole person, not a set of billable codes. About recognizing that healthcare doesn't happen in sterile exam rooms—it happens in living rooms, on front porches, and in conversations where someone finally feels heard.

What unites these efforts—whether we're responding to an overdose or managing chronic disease—is the recognition that healthcare is fundamentally relational. You can't build trust in a 15-minute appointment. You can't address trauma-informed care from behind a desk. You have to meet people in their space, on their terms, and be willing to show up again and again until they believe you're not going anywhere. That's community paramedicine. That's the work.

Evidence & Impact

Why this works

The evidence for community paramedicine isn't found in randomized controlled trials—at least not yet. It's found in the faces of people who didn't end up in the emergency room for the third time this month. It's in the phone calls from case managers who say, "I don't know what you did, but she's actually taking her meds now." It's in the data showing reduced 9-1-1 call volumes, fewer hospital readmissions, and overdose survivors who make it to their next birthday.

Our case studies document the tangible savings: emergency department diversions that save the system hundreds of thousands of dollars, chronic disease management that prevents costly hospitalizations, and harm reduction interventions that keep people alive long enough to choose recovery. But the real impact isn't measured in dollars—it's measured in relationships sustained, crises averted, and lives stabilized one visit at a time.

Since launching in 2019, we've built partnerships across healthcare, behavioral health, law enforcement, substance use disorder, and social services. Every referral we make connects to someone we know personally. Every warm handoff means a phone call, or in-person visit, where we vouch for a patient and ensure they're treated with respect. That's the infrastructure that makes community paramedicine work: trust, not technology.

{# Services Provided #}
Services

Services Provided

An overview of the core service areas delivered by the Community Paramedicine team in the field and in the community—clinical care, behavioral health response, post-overdose follow-up, care coordination, and connection to local resources.

Medical Care & Clinical Services

Assessments, chronic disease management, and post-discharge follow-up.

  • In-home and field-based medical assessments
  • Chronic disease management (CHF, COPD, diabetes, complex multimorbidity)
  • Medication reconciliation and adherence support
  • Medication administration (including injectables per protocol)
  • Wound care (acute and chronic, including unhoused outreach care)
  • Vital signs monitoring, EKGs, specimen collection, and lab blood draws
  • Post-hospital and post-ED discharge follow-up
  • Vaccinations (COVID-19, influenza, and other indicated vaccines)
  • Medical equipment assistance, setup, and patient training

Post-Overdose & Substance Use Disorder Services

Overdose response, rapid follow-up, and connection to treatment and supports.

  • 911-dispatched overdose response by Community Paramedics
  • Post-Overdose Response Team (PORT) follow-up within 72 hours
  • Field assessment and treatment of precipitated withdrawal
  • MOUD administration in the field (Buprenorphine, per DOH approval)
  • Naloxone education and distribution (patients, families, bystanders)
  • Warm handoffs to MAT programs, detox, and inpatient/outpatient treatment
  • Connection to SUD professionals and case management
  • Closed-loop follow-up with EMS crews and medical partners

Behavioral Health & Crisis Response

Crisis assessment, de-escalation, and co-response support.

  • Behavioral health crisis assessment and de-escalation
  • Dementia and cognitive decline evaluations
  • Welfare checks and high-risk follow-ups
  • Alternative response for psychiatric and behavioral health calls
  • Co-response with behavioral health clinicians and outreach teams
  • Support for patients experiencing psychosis, mood instability, or suicidality

Home Safety & Functional Support

Home safety evaluations and support for daily living barriers.

  • Home safety evaluations (fall risk, fire hazards, environmental risks)
  • Assistance with activities of daily living barriers
  • Coordination for home help, home health, or placement needs
  • Identification of unsafe living conditions requiring intervention
  • Advocacy during housing, placement, or eviction-related safety concerns

Care Coordination & Case Management

System navigation, follow-through, and closed-loop communication.

  • Navigation of medical, behavioral health, and social service systems
  • Case management for high-utilizer and high-risk patients
  • Appointment scheduling and appointment transport assistance
  • Prescription pickup and medication access support
  • Direct communication with primary care, specialists, and hospitals
  • Closed-loop communication with EMS, hospitals, and law enforcement

Social Services & Community Resource Linkage

Housing, food access, coverage navigation, outreach, and harm reduction supports.

  • Connection to housing and shelter resources
  • Food access and nutrition support
  • Transportation services
  • Insurance and healthcare coverage navigation
  • Legal and older adult services linkage
  • LEAD Fire participation and diversion-based support
  • Outreach to unhoused individuals and encampments
  • Harm reduction supply distribution (wound kits, hygiene items, seasonal supplies)

Public Safety & System Support

Reducing repeat crises and supporting response system capacity.

  • Reduction of non-emergent 911 and ED utilization
  • Follow-up on frequent EMS callers and repeat crisis users
  • Backfill and support for 911 operations during peak demand
  • Collaboration with law enforcement to reduce repeat welfare checks
  • Sharps container monitoring and public safety hazard mitigation

Education & Training

Training for responders and community partners.

First responder training in:

  • Behavioral health crisis response
  • Overdose response best practices
  • De-escalation and trauma-informed care

Community education:

  • CPR, choking, and first aid
  • Overdose recognition and naloxone use
  • Cross-agency training with healthcare and public safety partners

Services are tailored to patient needs, safety considerations, and partner capacity.
